    In actual use, it falls a bit short due to it's physical design combined with it's low-resolution imager. Most IR imagers are like a camera (or like a camera on a stick)--you point the front of the camera at whatever you want to see and look at the screen on the back. In the case of the Reveal, you point the front of the camera at whatever you want to see and look at the screen on the *top*. Although the case has a bit of a curve to it, it is not very ergonomic when it comes to figuring out how to point the camera at what you want to see while still being able to read the screen.

    This problem is exacerbated by the low resolution image and fixed focus. Unless you are far enough away from what you want to see such that there is enough information captured to give the image context, it's really hard to tell what you're looking at. Unfortunately from such a distance, it is often impossible to see the finer thermal details you are concerned with.

    Case in point: I was examining the manifolds of my radiant floor heating system to determine if the heat flow was as it should be. If I was close enough to the manifold to clearly see whether the individual floor loops were functioning, there wasn't enough visual information in the IR image to tell what floor loop I was looking at. If I was far enough from the manifold to see all the floor loops, the IR image wasn't detailed enough to determine if the individual floor loops were all working properly.

    While I could get the information I needed by very carefully counting the floor loops as I panned the Reveal across the manifold, the simple fact of the matter was, my higher-resolution Flir I7 showed me exactly what I needed to see immediately with no effort.

    I don't think the Reveal needs to have a higher resolution IR sensor (which would unduly raise the price), but it is definitely crying out for a decent resolution optical image overlay to add visual detail and provide context for the IR image.

    If you can't afford or can't justify the price for a more serious IR imager, the Reveal will do the job in a pinch, but it will take more work and effort to get the info you are looking for compared to a higher-end imager. If you intend to use an IR imager professionally, I would take that into account.

    Seek is doing a great job of making thermal imaging available to the common man, without dropping several grand on a FLIR or Raytheon. This little unit is a good standalone, but it does lack the features their smartphone attachments have, such as longer distance and clearer imaging, more color filters, and video recording capabilities. If they would have included the features of the extended range smartphone imager into this unit, it would have been much better. The flashlight is a good feature, but I would have passed on the light to beef up the thermal capabilities, were I the designers. It could stand to have a bigger screen. It's kind of like the Pontiac Aztec of thermal imagers. Their smartphone imager is better, but this one isn't bad at all for the price. One does need to spend a bit of time playing with this gizmo, so you can really understand what you are looking at on the viewscreen and what limitations you have with the unit. I am happy Seek is making such neat innovations in the thermal imaging world, and look forward to seeing what they might come up with next.
