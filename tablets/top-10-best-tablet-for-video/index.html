    I spent some time with both the Intel and this AMD version of the 2021 Win Max. I had left a review for the Intel version on another listing, mentioning a few things that made me want to test the AMD version to see if it would improve. Fortunately, it definitely improved and impressed me enough to want to keep this unit.

    My original idea was to combine my 2 separate devices (gaming laptop and GPD Micro PC) into one. The Win Max's form factor is the most appealing to me compared to basically all other handhelds since they do not have a touch-typing usable keyboard (ignoring the detachable keyboard of the OneXPlayer). I also wanted an AMD powered device since Intel's graphics drivers leave much to be desired.

    I have tested my unit with both Windows and Manjaro Gnome with Wayland. Windows currently offers a bit better performance and overally game stability with some of my titles (Risk of Rain 2, COD BO3, as examples). I will note that I had basically no issues with Linux using the AMD Win Max (touchscreen, brightness, volume, WiFi, wake from sleep and all inputs were working out of box), besides having to rotate the screen in the display settings. The Intel unit experienced difficulties with wake from sleep and random graphical artifacts. Different versions of the kernel and switching to X11 didn't fix anything for me.

    The XInput controls, keyboard, touchpad and touchscreen are all satisfying to use for general OS navigation and gaming. All gamepad buttons are clicky and responsive. The joysticks are admittedly limited in range, but they do serve their purpose well. I'm hoping that they don't exhibit drift issues anytime soon. Triggers and bumpers are using mouse button switches that feel nice, but sacrifices analog trigger functionality. I don't play any racing games, so it's not a big deal for me.

    The screen is exactly the same panel as on the Intel unit, so you should expect some slow pixel response times causing noticeably smeary looking motion. It's easy to get used to, but definitely not clear compared to the majority of panels on the market. It's enough for me to ding 1 star.

    Battery life is competitive with other similar handhelds. As far as I know, this device may have the largest battery capacity out of the x86 handheld market (I would need to double-check about the OneXPlayer 1S, which may be a bit smaller). My unit came with the "Normal" 20w TDP setting, and I chose to keep ot there for all my usage. I haven't found myself struggling with the battery life, since most games I play are relativley lightweight. The lowest I have seen is roughly 2 hours in games like BO3 and Valheim (by the way, Valheim offers more performance under Linux!). That is still on 20w TDP, so it could definitely be improved by lowering that to 15w and dropping some settings, or even limiting framerstes to 30. When I have it attached to my desk setup with a USB C dock, it feels like a very reaponsive and powerful mini desktop that can still game. Do note that USB C display out isn't officially supported, so you will need to use the HDMI port.

    Overall, I can recommend this product to others who are looking for a truly powerful laptop in a very portable size with decent built-in gamepad controls. It's a niche product with a high price tag that doesn't compete well with normal laptops of the same price range, so if you aren't specifically after this type of product, then it will not be worth it. For me, it definitely serves its purpose well and satisfies everything I wanted out of it.

    Display

    Flipping this device around, On the front, you’ve got an 11” 1600x2560 display. While the bezels on this device are slightly big for 2020, the display is still gorgeous and I love streaming content and playing games on its 120htz display. And the bigger bezels make it easier to hold on the hand without interfering with the view too much. In addition to being good for leisure, the display gets bright enough to read outside, so I could see this being used at any time of day, for just about anything you can imagine. I highly recommend a case or stand for this device because the edges are sharp and after long use, it's not comfortable to hold in the hand.

    The 8000 mAh battery on here is also gonna make sure that this tablet goes all day. I typically get a full day of use on productivity days but when I add gaming or streaming to the mix, I do have to keep a portable charger with me.

    If you look around the sides, you’ll see four stereo speakers that get pretty loud and have clear audio quality, but you won’t see a headphone jack. Bummer.     Storage/Expandability

    The base model of this device comes with 128GB and it’s an additional $80 for the 256GB model, and the S8 does come with a micro SD card slot, fortunately.

    Cameras

    The Galaxy Tab S8 has two cameras on the back, a 13mp wide-angle lens and a 6mp ultrawide lens. Photos and video are solid for a tablet, I was surprised to see that this ships with 4k at 60fps. That’s really the most important reason to buy this tablet... it’s probably one of the most powerful tablets on the market. Like I mentioned before, entertainment is terrific, but this might be the best tablet for multi-tasking I’ve ever seen.

    Samsung launched all their new tablets with a feature called Samsung DeX, that allows you to boot your tablet into a PC-like mode, where you can bounce between different windows of apps and move between screens easily.

    Samsung also partnered with Microsoft to allow you to run excel, word, and other Microsoft apps in multiple desktop windows. So typing on this tablet is phenomenal when you hook up an external keyboard or just a keyboard case.
    This feature is really the best part of this tablet and honestly one of its biggest selling points.

    In addition to DeX, is Samsung’s inclusion of the S-Pen in the box. Most students are taking notes digitally, so this is very much appreciated.
