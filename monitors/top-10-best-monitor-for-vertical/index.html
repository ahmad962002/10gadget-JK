    Disclaimer for fair context here:

    This is my first true 144hz, 1440p, IPS panel experience. Up until now, I've only ever had descriptions and seen photos (on less than ideal monitors) of what to expect. So my review here may almost certainly be biased in terms of extremes for both pros and cons.

    With that out of the way, let me just say.. wow, being my first experience with an IPS, I was absolutely blown away with just how much the colors explode off the screen compared to a TN panel. You get used to it very quickly but you definitely have those moments where you're just like "Damn, that looks really good."

    The exact same can be said about the refresh rate. If you're coming from 60hz, the difference is absolutely staggering. It's noticeable the moment you slide the cursor across the screen. It'll give you goosebumps at first. And again, it's something you'll get used to very quickly but will repeatedly be reminded of how smooth any and all motion appears at the most seemingly benign places. Especially with Windows 10 and it's very quick but subtle flow to almost any menu and interaction. Simply opening up the start menu or action center will just appear to glide in as though it was never on the screen but just floating above it.

    And finally the jump from 1080p to 1440p was much nicer than I had anticipated. My mind knew it was a half way mark between 1080 and 4K but my expectations were more inline with something more subtle. Nope. You're getting a massive upgrade in pixels here. The best thing about 1440p is that it truly is a half way sweet spot between 1080p performance and 4K sharpness when talking about gaming. The amount of extra power needed isn't insignificant, you will notice a loss in performance, however if you're rocking an RTX 2070 or equivalent GPU, it's safe to say that while you might not always get the best out of its 144hz refresh rate, you'll almost certainly exceed the 60hz you're probably accustomed to and it really does make a huge difference. 60fps just doesn't feel like a good performance anymore. It feels like what 30fps felt like on a 60hz monitor.

    Of course it's also worth mentioning a few "buts" now.

    The G-Sync on this thing is incredible. I can finally turn off vsync and not get those incredibly distracting screen tears which I'm unlucky enough to be very sensitive to. However it's worth noting that this is NOT a G-Sync monitor. It does not have the proper hardware to support G-Sync at its best. It's G-Sync compatible. Which is to say, it was built for FreeSync but after Nvidias driver update opened G-Sync to FreeSync monitors, this monitor was able to pass the certification process and be sold as being compatible with G-Sync with no real issues. The problem with this might seem nuance but it's worth mentioning. G-Sync will NOT work at framerates lower than 40fps. Once you drop below that, you'll start noticing screen tearing as the monitor doesn't have the hardware to stay in sync with the GPU at lower refresh rates. Not a deal breaker but just something to keep in mind. If you're still rocking older hardware, you might be lowering settings to accommodate the higher resolution at G-Sync speeds or resorting to turning vsync back on.

    Finally there's the IPS glow. It's there. It's absolutely there. It's both not as bad and worse than I expected but it's well within tolerance as I quickly get sucked into what I'm doing and stop noticing it. Unlike backlight bleed, IPS glow shifts around the screen based on your relative position and angle. It's easy to adjust the monitor in such a way that it's a minimal issue but due to the size of the display, it's impossible to sit at any position or angle and not see some IPS glow somewhere. Usually in the top corners as tilting the monitor up seems to offer the least about of glow.

    How much this bothers you it's going to heavily depend on what kind of content you'll be playing. Darker colors show IPS glow much more than brighter scenes. So brighter games like Spyro will hide it almost entirely, but darker games like Control can be real hit or miss and if you're not used to it yet, probably annoying. But it's a small price to pay for the kind of color you get over TN monitors.

There's a super short USB cord and a longer one that splits off. If your Mac is fairly recent it should be able to run off of one cable. My Laptop is a mid-2015 and it's able to be powered by one USB cable. So I have the single side plugged into the monitor and the split side connected to a USB hub. I found that this USB hub works wonderfully with this monitor - https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07G8CMR18?psc=1&ref=ppx_pop_dt_b_product_details if you're in need of more usb ports. This usb hub connects to your computer as well so you can use this monitor, a wireless mouse, even a phone(for itunes) with it. But if you connect it straight to your computer it should work fine.

    If you have your monitor set up it looks like it's turning on you see the AOC logo or you see an icon telling you to connect to your computer here's what's going on. If you see the icon with a usb logo it means that it's getting enough power but it's not able to sense the connection to your computer. Try another port. I have one port on my laptop that does work and the other doesn't. Go figure. If the AOC logo flashes on it may need more power or you may need to install the correct drivers. Now I didn't find the correct drivers on the AOC website but down the rabbit hole I was able to find out the type of driver you need to get this to work is from DisplayLink. They regularly update their drivers so google it and bookmark it. I can't post the link here but if you google it you should be able to find it. Make sure you are installing the correct one for your OS. Also, they have an excellent walkthrough on this website as far as making sure your computer will accept an application that's not an authorized Apple vendor. It's very involved. But after playing around with it, it should work.

    There is a problem with the monitor blacking out now and then it's happened to me. During and 8 hour day it'll flicker on and off three times throughout the day and usually the fourth time it won't come back on. I unplug and plug it back in and it's good to go. DisplayLink is aware of this and is working on a driver to fix that so keep an eye out for it if you're having this problem.

    The quality of the monitor isn't great it's a bit pixalated. If you're using a Mac Retina display laptop you're going to notice a drastic difference. I wouldn't use this for graphic design but if you are using this for coding it's perfect. I use it vertically for coding and it's excellent. It's not too big, it doesn't take up much space and it's portable. Four stars for the difficulty of set up and the screen black out issue.

    I think a lot of reviewers confuse "portable monitor" with "tablet". Yes, more recent portable monitors are more damage resistant. But a lot of the reviews are concerning a device they can throw into their car and drop a shopping bag on top of.

    This is one of AOC's first cracks at a portable monitor. If you keep that in mind and treat it LIKE YOUR DESKTOP monitor, you'll be fine. Don't load it into a crammed backpack. If you put it in your car, set it on the floor apart from other items rather than on a seat next to things that can damage it. It does come with a soft-cover, but it doesn't have a plastic shell plate in the cover to protect from strike damage. The monitor is larger than I expected, so it doesn't fit a laptop sleeve bag at all. The soft-cover is a nice accessory included with the device. It is NOT slim to today's standards, the display is the same average thickness as a Desktop Monitor. It does come with a built-in kickstand, so no magnetic flaps to worry about holding the display up. The kickstand has a single detente, with enough give after that to position the display from a 15 to 30 degree angle. If you go under the detente, it collapses rather easily, so I wouldn't press it. In fact, it's best to use this on a desk or table, as any three point stand isn't suitable for uneven surfaces. This isn't something you're going to use on a coach flight. Let alone a train ride.

    I got a monitor new in box, so I didn't have to worry on getting the wrong model. The resolution is true to size, and I was impressed how clear it is. The Surface is a high-DPI display, so I ended turning the App/Font size down to 100% on that display to get a useable output (Surface still runs at 150-200% independent of the monitor, so there's some drag-shift when you move a window over. That isn't a big deal, but it might turn off others expecting the same resolution on both -- it's not going to happen with an older display). The USB port didn't come out of the factory loose. The cable it came with is a wedge cable (1 USB-A to 2 USB-As) which I really don't like, but AOC said on the manual you don't have to use both. The second connector on the wedge is meant to supply more power for older laptops on the early USB 3.0 Standard before Superspeed and 3.1 was released. For my Surface Tablet, I used it with the charger on (works great) and off (drains the battery a little faster) but I'll probably use this next to a wall plug when I need it anyway.

    But the advice from other users is accurate: Windows 10 means auto-pivot stops working (Windows has Portrait/Landscape settings on the Settings panel, this isn't really a big deal.) DisplayLink drivers are best for a Windows 10 system, so install those first (which is a good idea for any USB Display Adapter, really), and find a DDC program to change brightness and contrast how you like it. (There are no buttons on the monitor, not even power. Unplug to turn off.)

    I bought this for use as a second monitor when someone else in our household is using our docking station with a full size second monitor. I have Windows 10 on my work laptops and Windows 11 on my home laptop and tablet. This monitor works fine with both of them - simply plug and play. The picture quality is decent considering the price. I do wish it had a few buttons for adjusting video settings like color and contrast. I'm sure there is a way to do it with the software they provide, but I haven't taken the time to figure it out. The quality is good enough out of the box, but If I had an easy way to change it, I'd probably tweak it. The same goes for the feature that allows you to rotate the monitor to portrait mode - you need to mess around with software for that to work, and I haven't bothered with that yet.

    The monitor comes with a USB type A connector which is fine for many laptops and other devices that have type A connectors. If you have a newer laptop or device that only has the newer type C USC connectors, you'll have to use a USB A to C adapter. I use an inexpensive one I bought on Amazon and it works fine.

    I don't travel with this monitor, so I can't speak to its durability when packed in a tight bag. The monitor back and frame seems to be very flexible and not particularly rigid or durable like a high end laptop. That's probably to be expected in this price range though. If you're planning to travel I'd recommend either not packing it in an overstuffed bag or else spend a bit extra for a higher end monitor.

    For the price range, this is a good basic second monitor.
