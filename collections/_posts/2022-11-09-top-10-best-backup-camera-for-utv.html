    Once you get an image it's nowhere near as crisp as what's shown on the listing. It's OK, but doesn't by any means have the contrast, detail or color depth the listing shows, regardless of any adjustments that can be made to the monitor's display. I haven't tested it at night yet, so can't comment on any aspects of that. The screen also needs to be mounted so you can view it dead on, otherwise a slight angle can make the screen image dim quickly.

    The backup lines are just that: strictly for backup. I've seen some cameras that allow you to turn them off or on whether you are in reverse or drive, but not this system. You would need to somehow connect the "reverse trigger" wire to be energized at all times. However, that would probably also continuously energize the night vision LEDs and burn them out well ahead of schedule.

    Why would you want the backup lines on when you are driving forward? Because the biggest weak link of this system is the extraordinarily wide angle view of the camera, and the backup lines MIGHT give you some rough estimate of how close behind you other traffic is. Having used this system several times now, I can clearly state that its only real value (at least as an RV rear view camera) is in detecting whether there is someone directly behind the camper or not. For traffic in a lane to your left or right, the camera view is so sweeping that by the time a vehicle is close enough to you to be able to identify it on the monitor, it's already practically right on the camper's rear bumper. What appears as a dime-sized blob on the screen (Is that a car or a truck?) is actually so close it's already fully visible in, and fully filling, your side view mirror. You really cannot safely use it to determine if it's OK to change lanes or not unless you see absolutely nothing at all on the monitor (or in your mirrors). That may be OK on the open highway, but once you get in town and everyone is tailgating or cruising in someone's blind spot, this camera will definitely not give you peace of mind when changing lanes.

    I thought perhaps changing the "zoom" setting to 4:3 instead of 16:9 might help, but all that does is prove that the 16:9 setting merely stretches the 4:3 image to fill the screen. There is absolutely no different in the image content itself. I was hoping I could actually zoom the image to make it more closely resemble real life, but the "zoom" feature is just a toggle between 4:3 and stretched 4:3.

    The optics and usefulness of this camera MAY be different if I mounted it much lower on the camper, but I doubt it. It needs to be high enough that a car behind you doesn't take up the entire screen and you need to be able to see what's BEHIND that close car. If you just want to know if someone is behind you in your lane, this system is OK. If you want to rely on it to see if it's safe to CHANGE lanes, don't. The image is just way too wide-angle to be useful for that.

    I rolled out a tape measure and had my wife stand at 25' behind the camper, then put small strips of white tape on the monitor to line up with where a car would be at that distance. I haven't tested that out yet, but that would be the minimum safe distance for changing lanes IF the traffic isn't fast approaching. If this system had a more natural angle of view, that "calibration" wouldn't be necessary. I shouldn't have to be so fixated on the camera image, trying to determine what I'm really looking at to figure out where the nearby traffic REALLY is, that using this setup makes driving less safe, not more.

    You should consider this system to be an accessory to, and in no way a substitute for, your rear view mirrors.
