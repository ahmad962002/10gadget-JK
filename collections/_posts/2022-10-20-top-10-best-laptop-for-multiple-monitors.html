    This is a preliminary review of a pretty decent screen. (Updated with a comparison to the Viotek LinQ P16C at the end.) I bought it for business travel and took it on the road once before I returned it. Quality is akin to what you'd expect to see from a panel in a ~$600 semi-premium laptop. I'm running it from the mini-HDMI port with power provided by either my 2016 Dell laptop's USB 3.0 port or the included 5V/2A USB adapter. No USB-C testing yet.

    It comes with three cables and a case. The cables are sufficient quality and resemble Monoprice's house brand products.

    USB A -> USB C (3')
    HDMI -> Mini-HDMI (3')
    USB C -> USB C (3')

    There's also a screen protector, but I don't know why you'd want it on a non-touch matte screen.

    The case looks and feels like an oversized version of $15 magnetic iPad folding cases. Quality is fine, but unlike those cases, this one doesn't attach to the screen. It's more like a binder or folio. It gently sticks to the screen's metal backing, but if you hold it open-side down, the screen may fall out.

    In use, there's more than enough friction to keep the screen up at a variety of angles in horizontal viewing mode. However, because the base of the screen has to rest on one of the case notches, there's always at least 1.5" of case protruding forward (or 3" if you don't fold it back on itself). If, like me, you position the screen to the right of your laptop and use an external mouse, the case will limit your room to move.

    There's no facility for portrait viewing, and this isn't feasible anyway with mini-HDMI; that cable exits one short side and the other short side has a protruding adjustment knob.

    Viewing angles are excellent. Typical IPS. Screen reflectance is also typical for a matte screen: quite low. The A/G coating, if it exists, is minimal and does not cause sparkling or crunchy text. No complaints about sharpness, though the onscreen control range seems to go from blurry (0) to correct (4, the max value), rather than the more typical blurry to oversharpened halo mess. There's a smidge of backlight bleed near the bottom of my panel, but not enough to notice in use. Linearity in both brightness and white balance is very good.

    Absolute brightness is lower than expected. The native panel white balance is a very blue 8800K, and that's where you'll see maximum output approaching 300 cd/m. Using the onscreen channel balance controls to bring temperature down to 6000K reduced this to 200 cd/m or so. More than adequate for indoor use with light control, but a bit dim if you're working near large windows. One annoyance is that inner bezel immediately abutting the screen is reflective aluminum; if you're viewing from even a slight angle in a lit environment, it distracts from the picture.

    A second annoyance is that the brightness tends to reset to 30 if my Windows 10 laptop goes to the lock screen. (It's been suggested that setting 'High Performance' in Windows power settings will prevent this; TBD. Battery life is likely to take a hit.) Extended sessions, it's fine, but CTRL-L and come back and it's likely to be dim again. Rather than fixing this with the fiddly adjustment joystick, you can install a program like ClickMonitorDDC to adjust the brightness with a keyboard shortcut. (A third annoyance is that the monitor doesn't correctly interpret DDC signals; sending '100' via the program sets brightness to 255 (off scale); you need to tell it to send 39.)

    Color gamut is bad: only about 55% of sRGB according to my EyeOne calibrator, though the dullness is uniform across all three color channels. Native color accuracy is very good. A forty-minute calibration brought the average error down to a stellar 0.7dE, though on previewing the profile, it was almost indistinguishable from the pre-calibration picture. It's a close match for my three 27" 4K LG screens and doesn't look out of place alongside them. Still, sRGB is the most common display gamut; if you edit photos or video on this screen, everyone else will see more saturated colors from the end result.

    The major screen controls are Brightness, Contrast, Black Level, Sharpness, and RGB controls for color temperature presets. Exactly what the second two do is unique to this screen. They both affect the white point, black point, and gamma curve. To get blacks and whites in the right place, expect to do some twiddling. I ended up at Contrast 40, Black Level 50, though the values were different when I drove the screen from my laptop.

    There isn't much overclocking headroom. I could only drive it to 63hz; not worth the bother. Input lag is minimal. Slightly less than my LG screens (despite running those 10hz faster) and barely perceptible with my wireless Logitech mouse. Power draw is modest: no more than 10W, and probably nearer to half that. My Dell's battery life dropped from 5 hours to 3 hours when powering the screen.

    To digress a moment to the actual utility of this screen: it's terrific. Windows 10's multiple desktops are no substitute for a second monitor. Probably +50% productivity. I'm so used to working with larger screens that I initially considered traveling with a real one at 20" or above, but that turned out unnecessary. 15.6" is perfectly adequate at the closer viewing distances typical of laptop use.

    That's it for now. I think this screen is good value. Good inherent picture quality, proper onscreen controls, and no obvious gaps in quality. I'd like it be brighter, but based on what I'm seeing from reviews of competing screens, there are a lot of pitfalls this one avoided.

    After using this one for a week, I bought the Viotek LinQ P16C to compare. The two panels are similar in brightness and quality. Similar limitations in color gamut and portrait viewing, and I assume (based on other reviews) the same quirk with resetting brightness. However, the Viotek is a bit smaller, has a thinner and non-reflective bezel, and has a case stand with more adjustment range that doesn't protrude forward. The only thing I don't like (on first impression) is the piano black rear facade that'll inevitably become scratched in use. Otherwise, I'd take it over this LePow.
