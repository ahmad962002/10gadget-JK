    The one downside to this system is that there is no way to actually attach to the system if you do not have access to the Internet. Arlo is completely designed around cloud computing, and cloud storage. This means that there is no possibility of using the system if you do not have the ability to connect to the Internet; the base station must be able to get out to the Internet. It also means that the Internet of things rules apply to this device: you probably want to separate your network from your appliances and make sure the Arlo device is not attached to your internal home network; that is to say that you should have two separate VLANs for all of your traffic at your house, with one VLAN going to your internal sensitive network, and the other going to all of your appliances. For many people this will not be an understandable concept right off the bat, but it's probably something to consider given the complexity of the Internet of Things appliances.

    ( For example, my TiVos, TV sets, Arlo, and guest wireless network are separated out from my home computer network. They are physically different network switches, which prevents them from talking to or infecting my home computers. Most providers do not patch their appliances for CVEs - aka common vulnerabilities and exposures.)
