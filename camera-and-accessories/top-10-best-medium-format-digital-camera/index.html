    Medium format is in a completely different league than full-frame digital cameras. A 50 megapixel Nikon or Sony full frame camera cannot approach the 50 megapixels from a medium format sensor. In medium format, each of those pixels is huge compared to a full-frame camera. The result is images with inherently vivid colors, beautiful tonal detail, exceptional dynamic range, and resolution at large print sizes (or with substantial cropping) that full frame does not achieve. Coming back to the car analogy, a BMW can be sporty and agile, but it's not designed and tuned from the ground up as a sports car like the Lamborghini.

    A medium format camera is not for lightning fast autofocus tracking of athletes or race cars. It is not as easily used for long telephoto wildlife photography. The format has limitations like any camera format. However, for a wide range of uses it provides superb image quality that is a real joy and surpasses anything that can be achieved by smaller formats.

    I think Fujifilm made the right decision not to come out with a full frame camera. The GFX 50R is comparable to a full-frame professional SLR in size, and runs laps around those camera in image quality (with the trade-off of tracking fast subjects and shooting at extremely high frame rates.)

    DxOMark’s subjective measurements rate the D750 very high on their 10 Best Sensors list, so I figured a series of subjective, Image Quality (IQ) only, test shoots of our D750 camera versus our other Nikon cameras (D800, D3200) in this resolution range (20+ MP) should prove pragmatically useful for fellow Nikonians, who may consider buying this D750. Overall I would have given D750 5 stars, but there is a concern that keeps it from a perfect 5 (see end of review). Here are the highlights from my results:
    - Lenses: I selected a representative set of Nikkor lenses from our lens repertoire, consisting of 2 Primes: 85mm f1.4 G and 50mm f1.4 G; and 2 zooms 24-70mm f:2.8 G and 70-200mm f2.8 G VR II.
    - Prep’ing the camera: D750 is calibrated for all lenses.     - Shooting conditions: With the (short) time I had for my tests, the test conditions were limited to Outdoor scenes with natural sunlight (in the shade as much as possible) with White Balances correctly set according to the conditions (Sunny, Cloudy, Shade). All shots (20+ shots per camera per lens) were taken wide open (f1.4 and f2.8, respectively) in Aperture Priority mode at the lowest ISO’s (100 for D750 and D800, and 200 for D3200) for best quality. Because of the very narrow depth of view at these wide apertures, I noticed that in continuous focus modes, D750 tends to “hunt” for a focal point incessantly more often than the other two cameras; so, after this discovery, all tests had to be reset to single focus point focus on all cameras to ensure fairness in comparing sharpness. Matrix Metering was used on all cameras. As in a well-known fact about Nikon cameras, compensation for exposure metering was necessary, I had to turn down 0.3 to 0.7 EV for D750 (0.3 EV for D800 and 1 EV for D3200) to avoid over-exposures and clipping in the R, G, B channels. All other special processing features (such as D-lighting, HDR, etc.) were OFF.
    - Results were checked with Photoshop CS6, Nikon NX2 and open-source RAW Therapee softwares.
    - Sharpness: Since this is tied to the camera sensor resolution, it was no surprise that the D800 out-resolves the D750 (and D3220); but it was surprising that the D3200 keeps up with the D750 quite well in most (if not all) shots. At Pixel (100%) and near-Pixel (50 % or 75%, depending on the software) views in all softwares, both D750 and D3200 are very similar on sharpness (I mostly checked this on slanted edges of objects, which show the gradual changes in straight lines, captured in the pictures); but when compared to D800, D750 and D3200 pictures taken with the two zooms were slightly less sharp and D750 and D3200 pictures taken with the two primes were clearly not as sharp as those taken with D800, especially with the 85mm prime.
    - Overall Brightness and Contrast (all lenses): With as much light coming through the lenses as possible, the sensitivities of the sensors do show differences: D750 resultant shutter speeds are almost always the same as D800, whereas D3200 tend to need one-step slower shutter speeds. Contrasts are almost identical between D750 and D800 and both visibly superior to D3200; except in very low light in-the-shade shots, both D750 and D3200 tend to go “dark” more often than D800.
    - Color-Depth Resolution: I checked this in Neutral Picture Control settings and with pictures that have gradual changes in the same color (for example flowers) by sampling the color code variations (in Photoshop) and looking for uniformity in changes from pixel to pixel. Surprisingly, D3200 keeps up with D750 very well, while both could not match D800’s superb linearity.
    The biggest concern with D750 is that I found (with all shots as measured in Matrix Metering in all cameras and RGB histograms verified for “no clipping”) D750 is prone to have red-channel blowout results in all Picture Control modes other than Neutral, especially with flowers; whereas this is almost non-existent with D800 and D3200. The red-colored flower shots with D800 and D3200 clearly resolve the tiny “veins” and the nuances in color graduality of the petals (albeit D800 is superior to D3200), but similar D750 non-Neutral-Picture-Control shots tend to smear a single Red color over the most of flowers’ surfaces. This issue is not new for D750 (see, for example, Flickr D750 group discussions) and can be “corrected” by a few methods (stay with Neutral Picture Control and do post-processing; adjust Red channel in RAW (.NEF); adjust Contrast, Brightness, Saturation, Hue individually in D750’s Picture Control).
    In summary: D750 is an excellent performer that outshines Nikon consumer-grade cameras, and, except for the red-channel blowout concern/issue, which can be “corrected”, D750 is a great camera for us to have as a backup to our D800.
