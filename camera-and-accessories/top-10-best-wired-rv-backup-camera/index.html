    Here are some very helpful hints if you plan to do this yourself:
    1. The actual backup camera must be installed right-side up. Which way is that you ask? The adjustment screws for the camera must be facing downwards.
    2. The video cable (yellow RCA plugs on either end) also has a red wire that runs alongside (not pictured in the product photos!). Do not ignore as others have done, as this is used to automatically power on the monitor when reverse is engaged.
    3. The camera has two leads off of it; one is yellow, and this is the video feed that will connect to the long video cable to the monitor. The other is red - this will be your power source to the camera. It has a corresponding plug that ends with a black & red wire. Take the red wire and splice into the power lead to your reverse light, and connect the black wire to a ground source (tap a screw on your vehicle's frame).
    4. Remember the red wire that runs along the video cable? One end of this should ALSO tap into the powered line of the reverse light - WITH the red wire from the camera power feed (as noted above). Note that you will almost certainly require extra wiring in order to accomplish this, so have it on hand before the install. It simply isn't long enough and ends too close to the video RCA plug. The other end will be spliced to the green wire from the monitor (this wiring bundle is also not shown in any of the product pictures here).
    5. Before ordering this system, look over the interior of your vehicle and try to determine where you will run the video cable. You could opt to run it beneath the carpet floor on up to the front of the car - or look for a cable channel found in a lot of cars - this can usually be found in the bottom of the car's door frame sills. If you do run the cable underneath carpet - make sure you have a wire fisher; this will help considerably. You can also remove carpeting, but I don't recommend this...
    6. Both the mounts for the monitor are a little flimsy (there is a square nut in both mounts along with a tightening wheel - DO NOT OVERTIGHTEN or you will crack the back casing of the monitor).
    7. The easiest way to power the front monitor is by using the cigarette lighter socket and a plug is provided; just splice the corresponding wires together. Otherwise, you will either need to find a 12volt power lead inside the car (fusebox or even tap the cigarette lighter from behind).
    8. You may need to be prepared to drill a hole somewhere near the rear license plate in order to run the camera's cables into the interior of your vehicle; this may be vehicle dependent but again, be prepared. Look at your license plate beforehand and imagine running the camera's two cables (power and video) to the inside for a better idea.
    10. Be prepared to remove interior panels (most likely in the trunk) in order to route wiring. Usually it's just plastic plugs that pop out (gently use a flat-bladed screw driver) and will pop back in.

    * I ran a good quality shielded RCA cable from rear backup camera yellow plug to front yellow plug on monitor wiring harness. There are two up front - only one fits - the AV2.
    * A separate trigger wire taps off the backup light +12v and runs to the green wire on monitor harness. Check which backup light wire is +12v with a cheap meter. Put one meter probe (black) to bare metal on car frame like trunk latch. Then use the red probe to see which backup light wire is +12v. The +12v backup wire will cause meter to show a voltage value when in reverse (be careful). That is your trigger connection.
    * Also connect the red on the backup camera to the +12v on the backup light. When the backup light comes on in reverse, the camera has power and the monitor is triggered on.
    * Ground (black) is to car body at the camera and up front at the monitor harness. Must be bare metal.
    * An add-a-circuit fuse box tap gives the monitor +12v ignition on. These are little plugs that replace a fuse. Find your ACC fuse slot, plug in the add-a-circuit, put two new fuses on the add-a-circuit (I used 10amp) and run the red wire from the add-a-circuit to the monitor wiring harness red wire. When the ignition is on, the monitor has power.

FAQS

Which is better wired or wireless backup camera?

There is no definitive answer as to which is better, wired or wireless backup cameras. Each have their own advantages and disadvantages. Wired backup cameras are typically cheaper, more reliable, and have a longer battery life. However, wireless backup cameras are easier to install and can be moved from car to car.

What should I look for in an RV backup camera?

There are a few things that you should look for when shopping for an RV backup camera. The first is good picture quality, so you can see what is behind your vehicle clearly. The second is a wide field of view, so you can see as much as possible when backing up. The third is a durable design that will withstand the elements.

How do you install a wired backup camera in an RV?

There are a few different ways you could wire up a backup camera in an RV. You could run the camera power and signal through the existing RV wiring, or you could use a battery powered backup camera.

Are RV backup cameras worth it?

Backup cameras in RV's can provide an additional level of safety while driving. They can help to avoid accidents by giving the driver a better view of what is behind them.
