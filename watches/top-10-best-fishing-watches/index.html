    On the "good" side, the watch can be set up to automatically go back and forth between the altimeter and barometer function. By way of background, in order to use a barometric altimeter, you need to calibrate it regularly since the barometer will go up or down depending on the weather. You can do this by either setting the watch to the current sea level barometer reading, OR setting the altitude to a known waypoint (i.e. the elevation of your house above sea level). Once you do that, the watch knows that "at this time, the barometer reading is X and the corresponding elevation is Y. Over time, you might travel uphill or downhill which will be measured by the change in barometric pressure that the watch reads. As we all know, however, the weather can also change. Therefore when using a barometric altimeter, you periodically want to recalibrate it. This watch has a clever function that reads how quickly the pressure is changing. If it's changing rapidly, it will assume that you're climbing and will switch to altimeter mode. If the "elevation" is changing gradually (or not at all) it kicks into barometer mode and assumes changes are weather-related. As a result, the readouts stay more accurate over a longer period of time.

    On the not-so good side, the biggest fault are the instructions which are a little vague. For example, the factory setting for the display is VERY dim (almost unreadable in certain conditions). The instructions don't address it, but I found online a way to do a factory reset, and one of the settings allows you to turn up the readout brightness. Also, the to calibrate the compass initially, a "calibrate" readout pops up and you can slowly turn the watch to get the compass working. To calibrate later on (which you WILL need to do) there is no "calibrate" setting, and you just need to calibrate it by slowly turning the watch horizontally.

    Based on this, you never REALLY know how accurate the compass readings are unless you check it against another compass or a known direction....which kind of makes the compass function pointless. Once the compass is calibrated correctly, it's reasonably accurate (+/- 5 degrees vs. an accurate compass) but since you never really know if it's fully calibrated, it would be risky to use in a real world situation. When the compass ISN'T calibrated, I've had readings almost 180 degrees off.

    Overall it's a nice altimeter/barometer/watch, but not a good compass. Since it's a few years old now, you can find them fairly cheap, so at the current pricepoint, it's a decent buy.

The GPS/watch combo worked for several years until April when the band broke on my trusty Seiko and I lost it down a ravine.

    Seeking a lighter and more robust solution, I bought this watch in May and write this review after 5 months of active biking and hiking.

    Review:

    The Instinct has been reviewed, praised, admired and generally pushed atop the same high pedestal as other Garmin watches and discrete GPS devices. The Garmin legend was built with nail-tough-hardware but frustrating and needlessly complicated software. The watch manual is an easy read and details pretty much everything that you need for operation.

    This review will focus on user details and tips that are not detailed in the manual but may significantly flavor the decision to purchase.

    1. The AFR (Arizona Fusion Reactor) in the sky washed out the lcd display on every watch, camera, cellphone that I have used with one exception: this watch. The monochrome, transreflective screen on this baby was completely legible under 12:00 noon Arizona sunshine. Really, truly, no marketing BS – the watch face and screen text are perfectly clear. If you live in the southwest deserts, you know exactly why I shoved this factor to the top of the list and why a Garmin watch with this screen technology should be on the wrist of every desert rat.

    2. Constant ON Display. Hey, no need to flip the watch up or any other nonsense to read the screen. If you have ever owned a watch or fitness tracker that required some type of action from you to activate the screen, you will know what a RECURRENT PAIN that becomes after a while. Anytime you want to sneak a peek at time, date, heart rate … just move your eye balls.

    3. The fourteen day battery is - - - - a bit of a stretch. Possibly a couch potato tracking trips to the bathroom or something but seven day battery life for biking is real. OK – if you use the GPS and forget to turn it off, the battery life drops but with an hour of GPS per day you can easily get 6 days and 7 days if one battery bar remaining doesn’t cause you panic. Try going 7 days with your Apple or Android watch.

    4. No touch screen. Just a personal thing. I hate touch screens on watches – buttons are king.

    5. The Android phone application is polished, comprehensive and most important – it works flawlessly. The app is covered in Garmin documentation but several things are not sufficiently emphasized.

    a. Sleep app can be edited post data acquisition to correct bed/wake times.

    b. Body battery includes data on heart beat variability and exercise history. I thought the battery concept was hokey until I understood the science behind it.

    c. Activities are specific and comprehensive. For example, there are multiple biking activity categories that allow tailoring to the specific type that you enjoy. This is not just a road/mountain bike choice but subsets of each type. This is useful because the energy expended may differ within the subset and permits better estimation of actual effort.

    d. Data ported to watch is useful. An example is the weather information available on the watch. Not only are the current temp and conditions shown but a second menu shows the next 4 hours of weather and the next 4 days of conditions. Arizona weather is pretty boring (sun, sun, sun) but this is handy up north where conditions change frequently.

    e. Bluetooth is a beast. I do not know the “official” range but it pairs instantly with the phone and I have never lost connection. Sometimes the watch calls out to the phone (in my car) at night and they chat for a bit. I suspect they are discussing my girlfriend.

    f. Best of all – you do not have to use the phone application at all. The watch is completely independent and you can leave the phone at home during your exercise.

    Conclusion:

    The Garmin Instinct addressed my biking needs/wants/expectations. Just get over the fact that it doesn’t include the photo of your German Sheppard on the watch face, 100GB of music/video or Starlink.

    At the end of the biking day, with the GPS off and trip data securely on the phone, the Instinct LEDs continue to flash – tracking your heartrate and beat variations. It watches your sleep depth, duration and energy expenditures. It even reminds you of your wedding anniversary!

    Reading the reviews on Amazon, it is clear that some folks were not happy with the Instinct, however; in this rat's humble opinion, Garmin hit this completely out of the park and if I lose this thing in the mountains, I would purchase another one the same day (assuming I found my way home GPSless).

    I'm not a rugged outdoorsman, but I really like this watch. I was looking for a rugged smartwatch because my last smartwatch (Samsung Galaxy Active2) only lasted about nine months even though I have an office job and my idea of exercise is walking. I've only had this watch for three weeks and it appears rugged but time will tell. The Instinct does all that I want for a smartwatch to do and more, namely:

    1. I can easily view data I care about monitoring at a glance. In my case, I put time, date, steps, heart rate, and battery status on the home screen. There are numerous other options. No, it's not a fancy, color display, but I don't care about that. I can easily see what I want at a glance even in full sun.
    2. I can easily monitor notifications and text messages at a glance. When a notification, text, or email comes in, the watch vibrates and I can easily see what it is at a glance. If I decide to read the full text, email, or whatever, it's easy to scroll down and read the whole thing.
    3. I can easily set timers and alarms which I use regularly.
    4. The phone finder works well and it's easy to access.

    This thing has just loads of other features that I'll use sometimes. But this watch does well the main things that I want a smartwatch to do at a price that doesn't break the bank. A few other items of note:

    -The battery life is phenomenal. I wore it 10 days before I charged it the first time and I could have gone longer. I was down to one bar, so I went ahead and plugged it up at the end of the day. At the rate the battery had been draining, I probably could have gone another two or three days on that last bar. Personally, I don't mind plugging up a watch every night, but if you like long battery life, this watch has it.
    -I wear the watch continuously unless I'm charging it, including in the shower. No problems.
    -As you can see in the photos, I invested in the silicone cover for the watch. I also got a screen protector and silicone plugs for the charging port. All this extra protection is likely overkill but, like I said, I had a bad experience with regard to longevity of my last smartwatch. I also got a metal band just because I prefer metal bands.

    The only slight downside to the watch in my view is linked to what I consider one of its strengths. Because the watch doesn't have a touchscreen (a plus for several reasons in my opinion), everything must be done by pressing buttons and some of those actions aren't intuitive. Furthermore, the book that comes with the watch isn't that much help. But here's what I've discovered ... There are a multitude of videos online that explain anything you want to know about the Instinct. With the help of these videos, the things I do most often on the watch soon became second nature. If there's something new that I want to do on it, there's another video out there waiting for me.

What is a good fishing watch?
