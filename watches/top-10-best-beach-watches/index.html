    I've only had the watch now for a few days, so this review will stand as an initial impressions statement. I will, however, return and expand upon my review as time goes on. You can see this on my Casio W-735H review. I bought this watch so that I'd have a real diver style watch for our vacation to St. Lucia. I wanted something that looked and felt the part and could actually take some abuse in the water, but not make me cry if it breaks or I lose it.

    Initial impressions: Really beautiful color, I got the blue dial and bezel and it's an extremely deep, gorgeous azure. The size and heft are perfect for me, I have a 7.25" wrist and this fits perfectly. Any larger and it would look a little ridiculous. I'm not normally into polished cases like this one has, but I'm ok with it. The bezel is hard to turn, but that's actually a design feature. Dive watches are meant to be uni-directional and you really don't want them just freely spinning, it's meant to track usable oxygen in a tank, after all. I put mine on a NATO band and it fits the style very well, but I was not prepared for how good the stock bands are, they feel like very decent quality for mass produced plastic. The operation is silent, I'm sure that's from the density of the case and crystal, but it's totally silent, even when I held it up to my ear, I couldn't hear anything. I also have no idea how deep I'm going to take this watch, but the 200 meter depth rating instills confidence. I'll go nowhere near that, but it's still a nice feature.

    Gripes: Almost everything I have an issue with is subject to personal bias based on a design (artistic) choice and will not be applicable to most other people. First, I don't care for how small the Casio name and the Marlin logo at the bottom is on the dial, not a good use of negative space. Next, I also wish the lumed indices were slightly larger to help eat into the dial center a little more as well as the date window and number, I feel it's a little small. Again, a negative space design choice. I feel if the dial were balanced out just a little better, this would become my favorite daily wear watch. Very personal, small dings that really only matter to me. Some things that aren't design gripes? This thing really does attract fingerprints and it shows them off in all their detail, so I find myself constantly wiping them away. Also, you can get a pretty bad glare off the crystal unless it's pointed straight at your face. Lastly, the lume doesn't last very long, maybe 15 minutes before it's pretty well dead. In perfectly dark environments I'm sure you might still be able to see it, but not in partially lit, but still very dark areas.

    Overall impression after just a week: I really enjoy wearing and looking at this watch, the deep blue color catches my eye and I tilt and turn in the light so that I can see the ultra deep color through the crystal. And, for what it's worth, it's plenty accurate from what I can tell (quartz watches usually are). The value for money is just off the charts, even Bill Gates wears one and he can afford like...any watch...company, he can afford a whole watch company, he could afford a bunch of watch companies, multiple factories, the people to make them, stores...and yet, he wears one of these....so yeah....The value is there. Anyone who wants to get into diver watches should really look at these, give them a try and see if you like the style. There are also plenty of colors to choose from and lots of NATO straps out there to help complete the look, if that's your thing. Somehow, this piece manages to have it's cake and eat it to, and then go back for seconds. It looks amazing, works very well and yet, is so cheap that you wish you bought another one, just for fun. Other than my design dings, there isn't much to dislike about this watch. Right about now I wish I'd bought a solar model, but that's only so that I'd never have to change a battery. I'm also very surprised by the following that Casio has on Reddit. The Casio subreddit is loaded with people who love their cheap, yet incredibly accurate, amazingly tough and truly capable watches, and now, I'm one of them.

    Context. Two months ago, my Timex Ironman died. I like to go sailing, so I needed a new watch. That sent me on an unexpectedly enjoyable journey, exploring my options. After visiting numerous websites run by 'watchies' and reading countless reviews, I settled on the blue Casio Duro. Why?

    I had these criteria:
    1. Waterproof. Sailing makes this non-negotiable. 200M is just fine.
    2. Analog. I'm done with digital watches, that just show me numbers. I find it boring. Call me old fashioned.
    3. Accuracy. When I buy a watch, I want it to tell me exactly what time it is. This doesn't go without saying. A watch with an automatic movement easily loses or gains 2 minutes per week. This means that you have to adjust it very regularly (and still manually wind it at least twice a month). Quartz watches are highly accurate, i.e. they lose or gain one to two minutes per year. AND they are much less expensive. So why do people opt for an automatic? What they say is: loving the intricacies of the mechanism, the tradition, and the longevity, passing their watches on to the next generation. Well, my next generation looks at her fitbit or smartphone if she wants to know what time it is, so good luck with that. I suspect the real reason is ......love. True watchies are not satisfied with just looking at their watches; they want to touch them, work on them, play with them. And that is what you HAVE to do with an automatic.
    4. Looks. I wanted a fairly large 'dive watch' in my old age. Why? It just appeals to me. I also wanted a simple, uncluttered dial and bezel. The colors of the Duro blue are absolutely gorgeous and always changing. Hues of dark blue early and later in the day, bright royal blue in the sunshine, and black in low light.
    5. Price. I needed a 'beater' watch, i.e. cheap enough that it's OK if it gets badly scratched or lost. To me, that means a max of $100 (although I could be tempted into paying $150, maybe even $200).

    After my research, the blue Casio Duro is a clear winner, at an incredibly low price of under $50, with the Orient Kamasu as a more expensive ($176) runner-up. And I look at her fondly at least 80 times per day. Love after all...

    I like really like the Blue colour I chose for my second Duro.
    But that's about all I like for this particular Duro!
    This particular Blue Duro is so uncomfortable on my wrist vs my other new Duro which is just fine. I couldn't figure out exactly why. I suspected the lugs are not well aligned on the horizontal plane, so I flipped both watches over put a plastic rule over them and then ran my fingers along both case edges. Wow, what a difference, this Blue Casio Duro is way sharper all along the case edges. I changed the strap to a softer silicone strap but it is still irritating to wear. The engraving on the Blue Duro case back is far less legible, not nearly as deep or even vs my Green Duro where the engraving is very even, deeper and well executed. I think the case back on this Blue Duro may have been opened at some point because the case's engraving alignment is way off vs my original Green Duro which is perfectly square to the 12o'clock - see the photos attached. Also the bezel has a very tiny burr or nick in it at the 12 o'clock mark, see photo attached - which is almost invisible, but it grabs my finger when touched. For a new watch that is a tad strange. The other marks in there are dirt that I have polished off since I took the pics ( sorry about that ) It will probably be possible to buff that nick out with a soft buffing wheel and fine mesh cloth later. Also its not nearly as accurate good at keeping time vs Green Duro which is remarkably accurate. I set time every day, no need for the Green Duro to be reset its jsust fine. Please note - I do not think any of this is in any way the fault of the seller. At first I thought the watch was a very good copy, but it is the same weight as my original Green Duro at 92 grams. So, this one will now sit in its original box until I decide what to do with it. I may open it up and check the movement later when I modify my Green Duro. At this point it seems I just got a "runt of the litter" Casio Duro. But at the price I paid its not sheep stations, it will make a real beater watch or a gift. Sorry I couldn't be more positive about this one.
